# The unified world model — the one core architecture

*A first-principles answer to "what is the true 1:1 world-model architecture," reconciling the two paradigms
already in this repo (the SCM compiler in `ARCHITECTURE_WORLDMODEL.md` and the agent society in
`ARCHITECTURE_AGENT_ENGINE.md`) using only what EXP-040 → EXP-101 actually proved.*

---

## 0. The one correction that changes everything

Your thesis — *model the relevant slice of the world with real structured state and real transitions, roll
it forward at real time, read the answer off the terminal states* — is **correct**. It is the digital-twin
thesis and it is how every great predictive system works (weather, epidemics, orbital mechanics).

But there is one word in it doing all the damage, and fixing it resolves every tension in this repo:

> **"Fidelity" does not mean narrative richness. It means grounded, calibrated, structurally-correct
> richness. Added detail helps if and only if it is *observed*, *estimated-with-uncertainty*, or
> *governed by a transition calibrated to real per-unit-time data*. Added detail that is *guessed*
> provably makes the forecast worse, because guesses compound multiplicatively over a rollout.**

This is not an opinion. It is the single most-replicated result in your own logs, and it cuts **both** ways:

- **Guessed richness loses.** The full agent society vs one grounded LLM call, pooled n=87: **Δ Brier
  −0.0009, p=0.98 — exactly zero** (EXP-101). The two *most elaborate* arms — B7 persistent per-agent
  memory and B8 event-clock temporal rollout — were the **worst-scoring at the highest cost** (Brier 0.22
  @ 44 calls, 0.235 @ 69 calls, vs 0.166 @ 1 call). The maximal "every person is an agent" world **loses to
  a 2-parameter mean-field shortcut** (0.144 vs 0.123; EXP-083). Adding bandwagon coupling to GSS opinion:
  **skill −0.15** (EXP-053/061). Adding 12 variables naively: **+0.111 log-loss** (EXP-072). The open-domain
  `calibrated_readout` where the LLM invents elasticities *with zero declared uncertainty*: **AUC 0.509 —
  random**, skill −1.58 vs crowd (BACKTEST_FINDINGS, 660 questions).
- **Calibrated richness wins.** The *same* GSS opinion model, with the *same* 11 variables but each carrying
  a **data-calibrated elasticity with a confidence interval**, beats persistence **and every other baseline
  by +0.107 skill** (EXP-073, 133 no-cheat forecasts). And the punchline: **going from 2 variables to 11
  flipped a loss (−0.032) into a win (+0.107)** — *more* fidelity, but only because it was calibrated. On the
  6-domain portfolio, fidelity wins **+0.150 (opinion), +0.316 (adoption)** on modelable evolving
  populations. Modeling justices as *interacting* agents beats the independent composite on coalition margin
  **−19%** (EXP-055, 954 real cases).

So your intuition and the "less is more" critique are **both** right, and they are not in conflict once you
see the axis they actually disagree on. It is not *rich vs sparse*. It is **grounded/calibrated vs guessed.**
Model as much of the world as you can — the more the better — **provided every added piece is either pinned
to evidence or carried as honest uncertainty the Monte-Carlo integrates out.** That is the whole game, and
everything below is the machinery that enforces it.

Why guessed agents specifically add zero, stated exactly: when you cast *N* LLM personas from one LLM, hand
them one shared dossier, and let them "interact," you are drawing *N* **correlated samples from the same
prior**, and the "interaction" signal is itself generated by that same prior. **No information enters the
system that was not already in the one-shot forward pass.** You have decomposed one guess into many noisier
guesses and re-averaged them — strictly adding error at every stage, never adding signal. The *only* time
agent interaction won (SCOTUS margins) is when the agents were grounded in **real ideal points** and the
interaction was the **real generative process** (coalition formation). Grounded agents + real mechanism =
win; guessed agents + guessed interaction = zero. This is a law, not a tuning problem.

## 1. The core: one world state, one transition, *no question-router*

You are right that the top-level router (`front_door.py`: classify → panel **OR** society **OR** diffusion
**OR** parametric **OR** individual) is a cheat. It is a cheat for a precise reason: **it chooses the
mechanism at the *question* level, so each engine produces the answer alone, and a real question is never one
mechanism.** An election is candidates *and* an electorate *and* media diffusion *and* donors *and* polls
*and* the economy *and* shocks — simultaneously, coupled.

The fix is **not** "one mechanism for everything" (that was the agent-society over-correction, and it added
zero). The fix is to move mechanism selection **down one level** — from the *question* to the *component of
the world*:

> **The world comes first. Each *part* of the world gets the transition mechanism that matches *its* real
> physics. The parts are co-present and coupled in one shared state, and rolled forward together.**

There is exactly one top-level abstraction, and it is the one you wrote:

```
WorldState_t   ×   Intervention_t   ×   ExogenousEvent_t
        │
        │   T_θ  =  Σ  component-transitions, each the right physics, all advanced by the SAME real Δt
        ▼
WorldState_{t+Δt}
```

There is no `if question_type == …`. There is one loop. What varies per question is **not the engine** but
**which typed components are instantiated in `WorldState` and which sub-transitions in `T_θ` are active.**
"Diffusion," "electorate," "committee," "single-agent," "price process" stop being **exclusive routes** and
become **co-present sub-dynamics** of one world. The election compiles to *all of them at once*, coupled:

```
WorldState(election):
  entities:      {candidates, campaign managers, major donors}      → decision policy (goal-directed choice)
  population:    {voter segments w/ within-segment heterogeneity}   → compositional + opinion-diffusion update
  network:       {media outlets, platforms, endorsers}              → cascade / point-process diffusion
  institutions:  {registration deadlines, debate schedule, EC math} → deterministic rule execution
  numeric:       {polls (measurement model), economy, fundraising}  → fitted stochastic processes
  information:   {per-actor info sets: what each side has seen}      → arrival + belief update
  events:        {debates, jobs reports, endorsements, the vote}     → hazard + calendar, drives every step
```

The "compiler" (Stage ② of `ARCHITECTURE_WORLDMODEL.md`) is exactly the step that **constructs this world**:
it does not ask "is this a society or a diffusion question?" — it asks **"what parts of the world are
causally relevant to this outcome, and what is each part's real physics?"** and instantiates them. This is
your "the world should come first, the mechanism chosen for each part" — made literal. The mechanism *library*
you already have (`compiler.py`'s bracket / committee / electorate / single_agent / generic_scm; `mechanisms.py`'s
aggregation / contest / diffusion / arrival / whipcount / escalation / persistence) is not a set of competing
products — it is the **set of physics modules the compiler wires into one world**, one per component.

## 2. How a piece of state is actually stored — the answer to "arbitrary weights vs prose"

You identified the two failures precisely: (1) old versions assigned **arbitrary faux-grounded mathematical
weights** (`openness=0.35`, `k_social=0.1` — still hard-coded in `compiler.py`'s committee/electorate today)
→ bad accuracy; (2) the current engine puts **free-text 1-2 sentence "context"** into a prose dossier → adds
zero. Your proposed fix — *structured fields, each populated by a language explainer instead of a number* — is
**better than both, but still not sufficient**, because a language explainer the LLM generated is still a
**guess with no error bar, no provenance, and no calibrated effect on the outcome.** It is prettier prose. It
will add zero for the same reason the current prose does.

The correct representation is a **third thing**, and it is already proven in your tree (EXP-041/048/049/050).
Every causally-relevant piece of state is a **typed cell** carrying four things, never a bare number and never
a bare sentence:

```
StateVariable {
    value:       a DISTRIBUTION, not a point        (e.g. Beta / Normal posterior, or a categorical)
    provenance:  retrieved | observed | inferred | prior | default   (ranked: data ≈ user > web > llm > heuristic > prior)
    uncertainty: explicit — how much you actually know this value     (drives shrinkage AND Monte-Carlo width)
    dynamics:    { timescale, per-unit-time volatility }              (how fast it moves — for the transition, §3)
}
```

and its **effect** on the outcome is **never** a hand-set weight and **never** the raw LLM number. It is a
**calibrated elasticity with a confidence interval**, sourced in this priority order (your `calibrated_weights.py`
already implements exactly this):

```
effect(variable → outcome) =
    fitted-from-data           if a labeled dataset exists for this (variable, outcome-class)   [best]
    else pooled/registry prior if a sibling domain has fit it        (precision-weighted combine)
    else literature effect-size if a meta-analytic estimate exists
    else LLM-elasticity-prior  WITH an honest CI                     (regularizes, never asserts) [floor]
```

The division of labor is the load-bearing idea, and it is the resolution of your point-3 question:

- **The LLM's job** is to *infer the variable's value* and *propose a prior on its effect* — the thing LLMs
  are genuinely good at (reading a situation, knowing "budget freezes suppress raises"). It is world-knowledge,
  and EXP-049 measured it worth **several hundred labeled datapoints**.
- **The estimator's job** is to *combine the variables without double-counting* — because correlated variables
  (party ≈ ideology) naively **overfit catastrophically** (log-loss 0.92 vs 0.60 for one variable) until you
  decorrelate them into latent factors (EXP-048) and partially pool toward the prior (EXP-041). This is the
  step both of your failure modes skip.
- **The data's job** is to *keep both honest* — the flywheel (`prior_registry.py`, `learned_priors.json`)
  tightens every elasticity as outcomes resolve.
- **The Monte-Carlo's job** is to *integrate all the uncertainty* — an unknown value or unknown weight makes
  the forecast **wider**, never **biased** (`bayes_logistic.predict_dist`, `structural.py`). This is how you
  get a 500-variable world without 500 error sources: the 480 you cannot ground enter as **wide distributions
  the ensemble averages over**, not as 480 confident point-guesses. *Richness in structure, humility in
  estimates, Monte-Carlo over the rest.*

**Worked example — "will my boss give me a raise?"** You do **not** write `boss_mood = 0.7` (arbitrary weight)
and you do **not** write *"the boss seems stressed lately"* (free prose). You populate typed cells:

| variable | value (distribution) | provenance | effect (elasticity ± CI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| company budget state | *layoffs last quarter* → tight (0.8±0.1) | retrieved (news/filings) — **grounded** | strong negative, tight CI (fitted) |
| your recent performance | shipped 2 launches (0.7±0.15) | user context — grounded | strong positive |
| boss's decision authority | can they even approve it? (0.5±0.3) | institutional — inferred, wide | gates everything (structural) |
| review-cycle timing | is it review season? (grounded date) | calendar — observed | positive if yes |
| comparable-raise base rate | 3%/yr median at this firm/level | retrieved reference class | the anchor |
| your relationship w/ boss | inferred, **high uncertainty** (0.5±0.4) | llm — shrunk hard toward prior | weak, wide |

The estimator knows *budget state* and *company health* are correlated (don't double-count), shrinks the
high-uncertainty *relationship* cell toward the base rate, gates on *authority*, and integrates it all. **That**
is what moves you off the LLM's lazy "~3% base rate" and onto the real number for *this* case — which is
exactly your Harvard point (real odds are 70% for one applicant, 0.001% for another; the base rate is a lie
for both). The tailoring you want comes from **grounding the specific case's variables + the right structure +
calibrated effects** — *not* from simulation depth or richer personas. This is EXP-040's finding verbatim: the
edge over the crowd is **well-estimated grounded variables**, biggest exactly where the case is distinctive.

> **The reconciliation your repo needs:** the engine "constitution" (`swm/engine/__init__.py`: *"NO
> variables-with-weights… never emit an elasticity of 0.4 ± 0.25"*) **over-corrected**. It was right that
> *arbitrary* weights are poison, but it threw out *calibrated* weights too — and calibrated weights are the
> single biggest measured win in the whole project (EXP-073). The correct rule is not "no variables"; it is
> **"no *arbitrary* variables — every variable carries grounded value + honest uncertainty + a data-earned
> elasticity, or it enters only as width."**

## 3. The transition — per-component physics at calibrated real time

Your instinct on time is **exactly right** and is already proven here (this is Law 2 of
`ARCHITECTURE_WORLDMODEL.md`, verified in EXP-063). Stated precisely:

- **Real calendar Δt, not an arbitrary "t".** The world advances in real dated steps; "one day later" moves
  each variable by **one day's worth**. The engine is Euler–Maruyama (`structural.py`): `drift = rate·Δt`,
  `noise = N(0,1)·volatility·√Δt` (Wiener scaling — this is why uncertainty grows like √horizon
  automatically). Volatility is a **per-unit-time quantity calibrated to real data** (opinion σ≈0.031/yr from
  GSS), and the calibration is *checkable*: at the right clock the 80% interval covers the future **85%** of
  the time; a clock 2× too fast covers 100%, 2× too slow covers 53%. **Miscalibrated time becomes a visible,
  fixable defect** — which is exactly the discipline you asked for.
- **No fixed "2 rounds."** The number of steps is set by the **real event calendar out to the real horizon**,
  not a hyperparameter. `max_rounds=2` in `society.py` is precisely the arbitrary-T bug you're objecting to,
  and it must die.
- **Event-driven, and the deepest lesson: don't skip the *events*.** Here is the subtle, load-bearing result
  (EXP-033): between events, a belief is a **near-martingale** — *nothing* beats "it stays put" over 1–10
  days, and learned momentum/mean-reversion make it *worse*. **The movement comes from the events, not from
  the inter-event drift.** So "roll one day at a time and don't skip steps" precisely means: **step to each
  real dated event (debate, jobs report, delivery, meeting, deadline), sample whether it fires and its
  outcome from the event model, apply its calibrated impact, and continue.** You can absolutely roll weeks or
  months — *if* you sample the events across that span. You **cannot** just extrapolate a curve forward
  (that's the martingale trap). **Long-horizon accuracy is a future-*event*-forecasting problem, not a
  better-*dynamics* problem.** Your "don't skip steps or it messes up the long-run prediction" is right, with
  this sharpening: the steps that matter are the **events**.

`T_θ` is the composition of per-component transitions, each the right physics, all sharing that one clock:

| component | transition mechanism | calibration source |
|---|---|---|
| entity decision (small-N strategic: a committee, a negotiation, a single reply) | goal/utility-directed policy, sampled — **this is where grounded agents legitimately live** | grounded ideal points; validated on SCOTUS |
| belief update (on new signal) | Bayesian / bounded-confidence opinion update | per-unit-time rate, √Δt noise |
| population evolution | compositional rollout + horizon-weighted period drift | real composition data (census/GSS); EXP-045/046 |
| diffusion / cascade | mean-field or network S-curve, coupled (∂pᵢ/∂pⱼ≠0) | fitted contagion params; EXP-053 |
| institutional rule | **deterministic executable code** (a threshold *is* `if yes≥5: pass`, not prose) | the actual rule |
| numeric / price | fitted jump-diffusion (GBM + jumps) | realized volatility from real candles |
| event generation | known calendar + base-rate **surprise hazard** | EXP-077 (82% coverage vs persistence 3%) |

**Your two embedded questions on the population component:**

- **Segment weights** = the *share of the population each segment holds* (e.g. "suburban college-educated
  women = 18% of this electorate"), grounded to real composition (census / voter file / GSS) and summing to 1.
  These are load-bearing over time: EXP-045's entire edge over persistence comes from the population's
  **evolving composition** — a predictable structure a market price simply does not have. That is *why* a
  grounded population is not a martingale even though a price is.
- **Within-segment heterogeneity** = the *spread of the driving variables inside a segment*. Not everyone in
  "suburban college women" is identical — there's a distribution of ideology, attention, and turnout-propensity
  *within* the cell. If you collapse a segment to its mean, you throw away exactly the signal that makes
  bottom-up beat top-down (EXP-034: ~9–12% edge, **biggest on the most distinctive/heterogeneous groups**).
  So you represent each segment as a **distribution** (or a sample of synthetic individuals drawn from
  segment-conditional variable distributions), simulate individuals, and aggregate. The heterogeneity is what
  makes the aggregate more than a base rate.

## 4. The readout — the terminal state *is* the answer, and the moat is the *path*

- **The simulated state space is the answer space** (your strongest insight): champion-of-the-bracket counts
  *are* candidate probabilities; replies *are* response probabilities and delays; cascade sizes *are* the reach
  distribution; utilities *are* the action ranking. No separate "prediction head" — read it off the terminal
  states of the ensemble (K trajectories, linear in K, never enumerate 2ⁿ branches).
- **Always a distribution, split into reducible vs irreducible.** `variance_decomposition` (`structural.py`)
  runs the ensemble toggling the two noise sources: **epistemic** (your estimate error — *shrinks with better
  grounding*) vs **aleatoric** (the world's own randomness — *the forecastability ceiling no model beats*). A
  question is `forecastable` iff irreducible < ~70% of variance. This is why "NBA favorite 37%, and 58% of the
  spread is irreducible playoff variance" is a **more correct** answer than a confident "52%." Past the horizon,
  more fidelity **cannot** help — and the honest model says so instead of bluffing.
- **Multimodality → pivotal-branch conditionals, never a lying mean.** When one pivot makes the future bimodal
  ("if the Fed holds → 25%; if it cuts → 85%"), report the fork and P(fork), not the ≈55% mean nobody should
  act on.
- **Counterfactuals are the real moat.** A one-shot LLM can guess an endpoint. The unique value is
  `argmax_a E[U | do(a)]`: run the *same* ensemble once per candidate intervention and rank actions by their
  effect on the outcome distribution. This is where you already have a real, replicated win —
  **CMV best-message precision@1 0.739 vs 0.518 random (+22pt, EXP-069)**; semantic intervention captures
  **36.5% of achievable A/B uplift vs lexical 14% (EXP-056)**. Not "what happens?" but "what happens under each
  action?" — that is the product markets are worst at and you are best at.

## 5. The discipline that stops this from becoming another lightweight sim

This is the most important section, because it is *why* every previous rich sim failed and the *only* thing
that makes richness safe. These are not nice-to-haves; they are the load-bearing structure.

1. **Every variable must clear a bar to enter as a *point*.** Observable/retrievable, **or** estimable with
   calibrated uncertainty, **or** governed by a data-calibrated transition. If it clears none of these, it
   enters **only as width** (a wide distribution the MC integrates out) — never as a confident guess. This is
   the mechanical guarantee that added detail cannot add bias, only honest uncertainty. (The BACKTEST failure
   was exactly this bar being absent: the LLM emitted `est_sd=0, weight_sd=0` — zero declared uncertainty —
   and the "simulation" became a static logistic over confabulated features taken as certain. AUC 0.509.)

2. **Weights are calibrated elasticities with a CI — never hand-set constants, never raw LLM numbers.** This
   is *the binding constraint*, measured repeatedly: naive 12 vars +0.111 log-loss vs calibrated +0.015
   (EXP-072); GSS 2→11 vars flips −0.032 → +0.107 *only because calibrated* (EXP-073). Delete the hand-set
   `openness=0.35 / k_social=0.1` constants; source every effect from the fit→pool→literature→LLM-prior ladder.

3. **A direct-LLM baseline runs on every question, and the machinery must *beat it* on held-out outcomes or it
   does not ship for that class.** This is the anti-self-deception gate. It is the *only* reason you could even
   discover "the society adds zero" — because you measured it against exactly this baseline. Keep it as a
   permanent CI gate, per question-class. Believable ≠ accurate; the evaluator is the product.

4. **Every added component/variable must earn its keep by ablation, per domain — and the "regime router" is
   this gate, not the cheat router you hate.** Reframe it: you are **not** routing to different *engines*; you
   are **dialing fidelity** on one engine, up only where it has earned its keep. `regime_router.py` already
   learned the map: fidelity *wins* on population/diffusion with weak baselines (prior weight +1.2/+1.4),
   *loses* on macro/election/liquid-market where a simple baseline is strong (−0.8/−1.6), and defers when the
   irreducible fraction is high. That is a **calibration-driven fidelity dial**, philosophically the opposite
   of a question-type dispatch: it *always* runs the one world model, and chooses how hard to simulate vs how
   much to lean on the base rate — with the choice **earned on data**, not asserted.

5. **Grounding and estimation are the frontier — not enumeration.** Grounding is worth **+0.111 Brier
   (3–5× everything else combined)**; more agents ≈ 0. The bottleneck is *reading real content well* —
   semantic stance against the specific resolution criterion (EXP-047: 0.57 vs lexical 0.15), grounded values,
   per-actor information sets — not listing more drivers. Spend the compute there.

## 6. The honest boundary — where this beats markets, and where it can't

Your calibrated take ("beat markets to some extent… better causal models, bias correction, faster processing,
better counterfactuals; weaker on insider info") is exactly right, and your own data pins the boundary:

- **Liquid mega-markets, on the point probability: no.** They are efficient, professionals already run
  bottom-up sims, and the price impounds it (elections: skill vs crowd −0.19 to −0.26 across 127 questions;
  near-parity −0.062 at best, still graded F). Do not try to beat a liquid price *path* — it is a martingale
  (EXP-033).
- **Off-market / niche / individual-scale / no-market-exists: yes** — this is the whole value proposition, and
  bottom-up grounded simulation is often the *only* method available (your Mark Cuban cold-email, "will my
  boss…", a specific person's Harvard odds). No market has priced these; the LLM defaults to a base rate;
  grounded per-case estimation is the edge.
- **Direction, calibration, pivotal conditionals, and best-action: yes**, even where you can't beat the point
  — the implied *direction* is right at the calibration rate (0.6–0.8), and the *best action under
  intervention* is a question markets don't answer at all.
- **The crowd-unsure slice: measurably yes** (+0.089 skill where the crowd itself is uncertain).

So "1:1 world model that beats markets everywhere" is not the target and never will be (Law 3: much of the
social world is past its predictability horizon). The target is: **the right model per question, run at
calibrated time, returning a calibrated distribution with an honest reducible/irreducible split and a
best-action — genuinely accurate on the forecastable slice, and correctly saying "irreducible" on the rest.**
